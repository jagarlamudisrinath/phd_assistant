## The Problem

In the financial services industry, data quality isn't just a technical concern—it's a competitive advantage. Poor data quality leads to missed opportunities, compliance failures, and customer dissatisfaction. As a Data Engineer & Analyst at Lakeview Loan Servicing, I work extensively with mortgage loan data to support capital marketing and loan offer generation. One persistent challenge I've encountered is the fragmentation of our data validation and quality assurance processes across different systems. Our loan offer campaigns rely on pulling data from multiple legacy databases—customer profiles, loan histories, credit assessments, and property valuations—but each system has its own data standards and quality controls. This creates a significant bottleneck when we need to generate targeted loan offers quickly.

The problem exists primarily because our organization has grown through acquisitions over the years, inheriting disparate IT systems that were never fully integrated. Each legacy system was built independently with different validation rules, data formats, and business logic. While these systems function adequately in isolation, combining their outputs for analytics and decision-making often reveals inconsistencies—mismatched customer records, duplicate entries, and conflicting data attributes. This fragmentation extends campaign deployment from what should be a same-day process to 3-5 days and occasionally leads to targeting errors affecting approximately 5-8% of loan offers, which impacts both customer experience and revenue.

## The Solution

My coursework in the PhD in Information Technology program has equipped me with frameworks to address this challenge systematically. The statistical methods I've learned in DSRT-734 (Inferential Statistics in Decision-Making) have shown me how to quantify data quality issues and measure their impact on business outcomes. I now understand how to apply hypothesis testing to validate whether data inconsistencies are causing statistically significant errors in our loan targeting models.

More importantly, the strategic IT planning concepts from ITS-831 (Information Technology Import in Strategic Planning) have given me a roadmap for proposing a data governance initiative. Rather than attempting a costly full system integration, I'm developing a proposal for implementing a centralized data validation layer—a middleware solution that standardizes data as it flows between systems. This approach leverages my technical skills in data engineering while applying the strategic thinking I've gained from my doctoral studies.

The solution involves creating automated data quality checks using Python and SQL, establishing clear data stewardship roles, and implementing monitoring dashboards to track validation metrics over time. As I progress through the program, I anticipate that future courses in IT project management and systems analysis will further enhance my ability to execute this initiative effectively. By combining statistical rigor with practical IT governance frameworks, I can present a solution that addresses both the technical and organizational dimensions of this problem—transforming theoretical knowledge into measurable business improvements.
