**Prototyping**

What caught my attention in recent literature was Beaudouin-Lafon and Mackay's (2003) concept of "prototyping as a design activity" rather than just a deliverable artifact. They argue that the real value of prototyping lies in the iterative learning process it enables between designers and users, not merely in producing a working model. In my role as a Data Engineer at Lakeview Loan Servicing, I've experienced this firsthand when building analytics dashboards for our capital marketing team. We don't prototype to create a miniature version of the final system—we prototype to discover what the final system should actually be.

The authors distinguish between "throwaway prototyping" and "evolutionary prototyping," a distinction that resonates deeply with my work on loan offer generation systems (Beaudouin-Lafon & Mackay, 2003). When exploring a new data visualization approach for mortgage pricing analytics, I often build throwaway prototypes using simple Python scripts and Tableau mockups specifically designed to be discarded after they've taught us something about user needs. Conversely, our core data pipeline prototypes evolve into production systems because the underlying architecture was sound from the start, even if the initial implementation was incomplete.

What strikes me most is how prototyping fundamentally changes the requirements gathering process. Rather than attempting to extract complete specifications upfront—an approach that often fails in complex data environments—prototyping allows stakeholders to react to tangible examples. When I showed our marketing team a rough prototype of a new loan segmentation dashboard, their feedback revealed requirements they couldn't have articulated in abstract discussions. The prototype became a communication tool that bridged the gap between my technical understanding and their business needs, ultimately leading to a more effective final system.
