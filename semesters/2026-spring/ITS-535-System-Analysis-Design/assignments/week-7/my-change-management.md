**Impact**

What caught my attention in Kotter and Schlesinger's (2008) research was their finding that inadequate change management causes an estimated 70% of organizational change initiatives to fail—a staggering statistic that I've witnessed firsthand in systems projects at Lakeview Loan Servicing. They argue that the human dimension of change is not peripheral to technical implementation but rather the central determinant of whether systems projects succeed or fail. This resonates deeply with my experience implementing data analytics platforms for our capital marketing and loan offer generation systems, where technical excellence alone has never guaranteed adoption or business value.

The most profound impact I've observed is how change management directly influences user acceptance and system utilization rates. When we deployed a new mortgage pricing analytics dashboard last year, I initially focused entirely on data accuracy and system performance—the technical metrics I understood as a data engineer. What I failed to appreciate was the resistance from loan officers who saw the new system as disrupting their established workflows. Without proper change management, our technically sound solution sat largely unused for months. The authors emphasize that resistance to change is natural and predictable, not a problem to be overcome but a signal to be understood and addressed through communication, participation, and support (Kotter & Schlesinger, 2008).

I've learned that change management transforms systems implementation from a purely technical exercise into a sociotechnical endeavor. When we later implemented automated loan segmentation tools, we invested significant effort in stakeholder communication, training programs, and gradual rollout strategies. The impact was measurable: adoption rates exceeded 85% within the first month compared to less than 30% in our previous deployment. This experience taught me that the impact of change management on systems projects isn't additive—it's multiplicative, fundamentally determining whether technical capabilities translate into organizational value.

**Reference**

Kotter, J. P., & Schlesinger, L. A. (2008). Choosing strategies for change. *Harvard Business Review*, 86(7/8), 130-139. https://hbr.org/2008/07/choosing-strategies-for-change
