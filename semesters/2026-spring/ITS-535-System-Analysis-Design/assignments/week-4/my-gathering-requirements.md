**Interactive Methods**

As a data engineer working with mortgage loan data at Lakeview Loan Servicing, I've found that interviewing stakeholders is the most beneficial requirements gathering method in systems analysis and design. While questionnaires and document analysis have their place, interviews provide a depth of understanding that other methods simply cannot match. What caught my attention in recent research is how effective interviews are at uncovering tacit knowledge—the implicit requirements that stakeholders know but often fail to articulate in written form (Paetsch et al., 2003).

In my experience building analytical systems for capital marketing and loan offer generation, I've seen firsthand how critical it is to understand not just what users say they need, but what they actually need. Interviews allow for real-time clarification, follow-up questions, and the ability to read non-verbal cues that reveal underlying concerns or priorities. When I'm gathering requirements for a new data pipeline or reporting dashboard, a 30-minute conversation with a business analyst or loan officer reveals nuances that would take weeks to uncover through questionnaires alone.

The interactive nature of interviews also builds trust and stakeholder buy-in. When business users feel heard and see their specific concerns addressed during requirements gathering, they become invested in the system's success. This collaborative approach has proven invaluable when implementing statistical models or data-driven decision tools—areas where understanding the business context is as important as technical precision. While I still use document analysis to understand existing processes and data structures, I've learned that the human element of face-to-face interviewing provides irreplaceable insights that directly translate into more effective system requirements and ultimately, better solutions.
